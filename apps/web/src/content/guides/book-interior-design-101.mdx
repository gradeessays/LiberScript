Readers rarely consciously notice good interior design, but they notice bad interior design
immediately, even if they can't articulate why a book "feels off." Cramped margins, an awkward
font, inconsistent spacing, and orphaned lines at the top of pages all create a subtle sense
that something isn't quite right. Good interior design is largely invisible: it lets the text
do its job without the formatting getting in the way.

This guide covers the foundational decisions behind a well-designed book interior: trim size,
margins, typography, and the details that, cumulatively, determine whether a book reads as
professionally produced.

## Trim size: the foundation of every other decision

Trim size is the physical dimensions of your printed book (width x height), and it's the first
decision because it affects nearly everything else: how much text fits per page, what margins
look proportionate, and what your page count and spine width will be.

**Common trim sizes for fiction**: 5" x 8" and 5.25" x 8" are popular for mass-market-feeling
fiction; 5.5" x 8.5" is a common "trade paperback" size that reads as slightly more substantial.

**Common trim sizes for nonfiction**: 6" x 9" is the standard for most nonfiction, business
books, and memoirs, large enough to accommodate more text per page (useful for books with
higher word counts) while still feeling like a standard paperback.

**Genre conventions matter**: picking up comparable books in your genre and noting their trim
sizes is a quick way to understand reader expectations. A fantasy novel formatted at an unusual
trim size might look out of place next to genre peers on a shelf or in a "customers also
bought" carousel (where cover thumbnails are compared at similar scales).

**Trim size and page count interact**: a longer manuscript at a smaller trim size produces a
thicker book; the same manuscript at a larger trim size produces a thinner book. For very long
manuscripts, a larger trim size (6" x 9" rather than 5" x 8") keeps the page count, and
therefore the printing cost and spine width, more manageable. See our [print-on-demand
guide](/guides/print-on-demand-explained) for how trim size affects printing costs.

## Margins and gutters

Margins are the white space around your text block on each page. They're not arbitrary; they
serve functional purposes and have minimums set by your printer.

**The gutter (inside margin)**: the inside margin, closest to the spine, needs to be wider than
the outside margin because part of it is "lost" into the binding, especially for thicker books.
KDP Print and IngramSpark both specify minimum gutter margins that increase with page count;
a 400-page book needs a wider gutter than a 150-page book to ensure text isn't swallowed by the
binding.

**Outside, top, and bottom margins**: these don't have the binding constraint but still need to
be generous enough that the page doesn't feel cramped, and that readers' thumbs (which naturally
rest near the edges when holding a book) don't cover text.

**A practical margin starting point**: many interior design templates use approximately
0.75"-1" outside/top/bottom margins and a gutter margin starting around 0.875" and increasing
for higher page counts. These are starting points, not universal rules; your formatting tool
(including LiberScript) typically calculates appropriate margins based on your trim size and
estimated page count.

**Margins and readability**: wider margins generally improve readability by giving the eye a
clear boundary and preventing the page from feeling like a wall of text. But excessively wide
margins waste space and increase page count (and therefore printing cost) without benefit.
There's a balance, and genre conventions again provide a useful reference point.

## Typography: choosing and using fonts

### Body text font choices

**Serif fonts for body text**: the vast majority of printed books use a serif font for body
text (the small decorative strokes at the ends of letters). Serif fonts are widely considered
easier to read in long blocks of text in print, partly due to long-standing convention that
shapes reader expectations of what a "book" looks like. Common choices include Garamond,
Georgia, Caslon, Bembo, and many other classic book typefaces.

**Sans-serif for body text**: less common for fiction, but used in some nonfiction, particularly
books with a modern design sensibility, technical content, or a target audience where a
sans-serif feels appropriate (some business and design books, for example).

**Font licensing**: many classic book fonts require a license for commercial use, including
embedding in published books. Free, properly licensed alternatives (including fonts bundled
with formatting tools or available through services like Google Fonts for digital use) avoid
licensing issues. Always confirm a font's license permits your intended use, both for print
embedding and ebook embedding, before committing to it for a published book.

### Font size and line spacing (leading)

**Font size**: body text in print books is typically 10-12 points, depending on the specific
font (some fonts read larger or smaller at the same point size) and trim size (smaller trim
sizes sometimes use slightly smaller text to manage page count).

**Leading (line spacing)**: the vertical space between lines of text, leading is typically set
somewhat larger than the font size itself (commonly 120-150% of the font size). Too-tight
leading makes text feel cramped and harder to track line to line; too-loose leading can make
text feel disconnected and increases page count unnecessarily.

**Measure (line length)**: the width of your text block affects readability too. Lines that are
too long make it easy for a reader's eye to lose its place when returning to the start of the
next line; lines that are too short create a choppy reading rhythm. Trim size, margins, and font
size all interact to determine your measure, which is part of why these decisions aren't made
in isolation.

## Paragraph styling

**First-line indents vs. block paragraphs**: most fiction uses first-line indents (the first
line of each paragraph is indented, with no extra space between paragraphs) rather than block
paragraphs (no indent, with space between paragraphs, common in business documents and emails).
First-line indents are the convention for fiction; using block-paragraph styling in a novel can
look unintentionally like a manuscript rather than a finished book.

**The first paragraph of a chapter or section**: convention dictates that the first paragraph
after a chapter heading or section break is not indented (since the heading itself already
signals a new section, an indent is redundant). Many formatting tools handle this automatically
if you use proper heading styles.

**Paragraph spacing in nonfiction**: nonfiction with headers, bullet points, and other
structural elements often uses block paragraphs with spacing between them, since the structural
elements already break up the visual flow differently than continuous fiction prose.

## Justification and hyphenation

**Justified text**: most print books use justified text (both left and right edges of the text
block are flush, achieved by adjusting spacing between words). Justified text creates the
clean, even-edged columns associated with traditional book design.

**Hyphenation**: justified text without hyphenation can create awkward spacing, especially with
long words that would otherwise leave excessive gaps. Hyphenation breaks long words across
lines when needed to maintain even spacing. Most professional formatting tools handle
hyphenation automatically using language-specific hyphenation dictionaries; reviewing the
result for awkward or excessive hyphenation (many consecutive lines ending in hyphens) is worth
a pass during proofing.

**Left-aligned (ragged-right) text**: some books, particularly certain nonfiction and
poetry, use left-aligned text rather than justified. This avoids justification's spacing
adjustments entirely but creates an uneven right edge. It's a stylistic choice more than an
error, though it's less common for fiction.

## Widows, orphans, and page breaks

**Widows and orphans**: a "widow" is a single line of a paragraph that appears alone at the top
of a page (the rest of the paragraph is on the previous page); an "orphan" is a single line
that appears alone at the bottom of a page (the rest continues on the next page). Both are
considered typographic flaws in professional book design, and most formatting tools include
settings to prevent them by adjusting where page breaks fall.

**Chapter starts and page breaks**: convention in many print books is for chapters to start on
a new page, sometimes specifically on a right-hand (recto) page, which can mean inserting a
blank left-hand page if a chapter would otherwise start on the left. Whether to enforce
right-hand chapter starts is a design choice; it's more common in literary fiction and trade
nonfiction than in genre fiction, where chapters starting on either side is generally accepted.

## Chapter openings: drop caps, ornaments, and white space

The opening of each chapter is a design opportunity. Common approaches:

- **Drop caps**: an enlarged first letter of the chapter's first word, spanning multiple lines
  of text. A classic touch that signals "new chapter" visually.
- **Small caps for the first few words**: the first line or first few words of a chapter set in
  small capital letters, a more subtle alternative to a drop cap.
- **Decorative ornaments**: a small graphic element (a flourish, symbol, or simple line) below
  the chapter title, used consistently throughout the book as a design signature.
- **White space alone**: simply leaving generous space between the chapter title and the start
  of the text, letting the white space itself signal the chapter opening without additional
  decoration.

Whichever approach you choose, consistency throughout the book matters more than which specific
approach you pick. A drop cap on chapter one and plain text on chapter two reads as an error,
not a stylistic choice.

## Headers, footers, and page numbers

**Running heads**: many books include a running head (a small line of text at the top of each
page, often the book title on one side and the chapter title or author name on the other).
This is more common in nonfiction than fiction; many novels omit running heads entirely or use
them sparingly.

**Page numbers**: nearly all print books include page numbers, typically in the footer.
Front matter (title page, copyright page, dedication) usually doesn't display a page number even
though it's counted, and front matter is often numbered with lowercase roman numerals if numbered
at all, with arabic numerals starting at chapter one. See our guide on [front matter and back
matter](/guides/front-matter-back-matter-every-book-needs) for what goes where.

**Headers/footers on chapter-opening pages**: convention often omits the running head (though
not the page number) on pages where a chapter begins, since the chapter title itself serves a
similar wayfinding purpose on that page.

## Color vs. black-and-white interiors

Most fiction and a large share of nonfiction are printed in black and white (technically,
grayscale for any images, with black text). Black-and-white printing is significantly cheaper
per page than color printing on POD platforms, which directly affects your royalty math.

Color interiors are necessary for books with color photographs, illustrations, charts that rely
on color coding, or children's picture books. If your book doesn't need color for its content
to make sense, black-and-white interiors are both the convention and the economical choice. See
our [print-on-demand guide](/guides/print-on-demand-explained) for the cost difference between
color and black-and-white printing.

## Genre-specific interior design conventions

**Literary fiction**: often uses more generous white space, sometimes wider margins, and
typography choices that feel timeless rather than trend-driven.

**Genre fiction (romance, thriller, fantasy, etc.)**: typically follows established genre
conventions closely; readers in these genres have strong expectations shaped by traditionally
published comparable titles, and interiors that deviate significantly can feel unfamiliar.

**Nonfiction with structure**: business books, self-help, and how-to nonfiction often include
headers, subheaders, bullet lists, callout boxes, and sometimes sidebars. These elements need
consistent styling (a header hierarchy, consistent bullet formatting) that a pure-prose fiction
interior doesn't require.

**Poetry**: poetry has unique typographic needs around line breaks, stanza spacing, and
intentional white space that differ substantially from prose formatting; poetry formatting is
often closer to a design exercise than standard prose layout.

**Children's books and picture books**: typically use fixed-layout design (text and images
positioned precisely on each page) rather than reflowable text, a fundamentally different
design approach covered separately from standard prose interior design.

## Tools for interior design

**LiberScript and similar all-in-one tools**: handle interior design through templates that
apply consistent typography, margins, and chapter styling based on your chosen trim size,
exporting both print-ready PDF and EPUB from the same source.

**Dedicated design software (Adobe InDesign, Affinity Publisher)**: offer the most granular
control over every typographic detail, at the cost of a much steeper learning curve. Used by
professional book designers and authors willing to learn desktop publishing software. See our
guide on [LiberScript vs. Affinity Publisher and InDesign](/guides/liberscript-vs-affinity-publisher-indesign)
for a comparison.

**Word processors with templates**: Microsoft Word and similar tools can produce passable
interiors with templates and careful style usage, though achieving the level of typographic
control of dedicated tools requires more manual work and is more error-prone for things like
consistent drop caps, widow/orphan control, and precise margin calculations.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need to hire a designer for my book interior?**

Not necessarily. Many formatting tools, including LiberScript, apply professional interior
design templates automatically based on your trim size and genre, producing results comparable
to a professionally designed interior without manual design work. Hiring a designer makes sense
for books with complex layouts (heavy nonfiction with sidebars, illustrated books) or authors
who want a highly custom design beyond template options.

**What's the difference between interior design for print and ebook?**

Print interior design involves fixed decisions (trim size, margins, page breaks) that don't
change once set. Ebook "design" is more about structure and CSS styling that adapts to
different screen sizes and reader font preferences; an ebook reader can change the font size,
which a print book obviously can't. See our guide on [print vs. ebook
formatting](/guides/kdp-print-vs-ebook-formatting) for the full comparison.

**How do I know if my margins are too narrow?**

If text feels like it's running into the edge of the page, or if KDP Print or IngramSpark's
file checker flags margin issues during upload, your margins likely need adjustment. Both
platforms specify minimum margins based on page count; following their guidelines (or using a
formatting tool that calculates this automatically) avoids the issue.

**Should I use the same interior design for print and ebook?**

The underlying content (chapter structure, headings, front/back matter) should be consistent,
but the actual styling differs by necessity: print has fixed margins and page breaks; ebooks
are reflowable and adapt to reader settings. A good formatting tool produces both from the same
source while applying medium-appropriate styling to each.

**What if my book is much longer or shorter than typical for my genre?**

Very long books may need a larger trim size to keep page count and spine width manageable; very
short books (novellas) may look disproportionate at standard trim sizes with very few pages.
Adjusting trim size for unusual lengths is normal; see our [word count guidelines by
genre](/guides/word-count-guidelines-by-genre) for context on what's typical.

## The bottom line

Book interior design is mostly about consistency and following conventions that readers
recognize, even subconsciously, as "a real book." Trim size, margins, and typography work
together, and changing one affects the others. For most authors, a formatting tool that applies
professional templates based on trim size and genre handles these decisions well; understanding
the underlying principles helps you make informed choices when templates offer options, and
helps you spot issues during proofing.
